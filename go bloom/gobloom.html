GoBloom — Progress Report #3

1. Working Interactive System

The system is a timed gardening game. Five seeds appear on the ground and the player must click every seed before the sun crosses the sky in 10 seconds. Each seed randomly grows into either a flower (+25 points) or a weed (-10 points). If any seed is left unclicked when the sun sets, the player loses a life. The game ends when all three lives are gone.


Core rule: Click every seed before the sun sets or lose a life.

Multiple structural states: The system moves through a waiting state (press any key), an active state (sun moving, seeds clickable), and either a end state (reset and advance) or a game over state.

Meaningful interaction: Every click is a risk. The player does not know if a seed will become a flower or a weed until they click it. Clicking all seeds is required to survive, but weeds cost points, so the score reflects the quality of each run.

Visible evolution over time: Every two rounds an extra seed is added to the ground, and every round the timer shrinks by half a second. The garden gets harder to manage the longer the player survives.

2. Process Documentation

Iterative development: The system started as two fixed seeds that grew when the user pressed a key: one at a steady rate, one at a random rate. Over several iterations it was rebuilt into a click-based game with a scoring system, lives, and a moving sun timer.

Structural changes over time: The original height variable driving growth was replaced by an array of seed objects, each tracking its own clicked state. Plants became a separate array with a type property (flower or weed) assigned at the moment of clicking.

Code experiments: An early version used keyPressed() to grow both flowers simultaneously. This was replaced with mousePressed() and dist() hit detection so each seed could be clicked individually.

Reflections on rule changes: The original lose condition was only failing to grow a flower weeds did not count against survival. This was changed so that any unclicked seed costs a life, which made every seed feel important rather than optional.

Evidence of revision: The timer started fixed at 12 seconds, was later made variable per round, then locked back to 10 seconds with a -0.5s reduction per round after feedback that the difficulty curve felt uneven. The number of seeds also increases every two rounds to compound the pressure gradually.

3. What the System Is About

The system uses a garden as a metaphor for decision-making under pressure. Every seed is an unknown. The player cannot tell what will grow until they commit to clicking it. The sun is indifferent; it moves at the same pace regardless of what the player does. The only control the player has is how quickly they act, not what the outcome will be. As rounds progress, more seeds appear and less time is available, so the player has to make faster decisions with the same uncertainty.
